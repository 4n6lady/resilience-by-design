# Contributing to Resilience by Design

Thank you for your interest in contributing. This toolkit exists
because people who do this work — security and incident response
practitioners, team leads, managers — have knowledge and experience
that should be shared. If something here doesn't reflect the reality
of your team, or if you see a gap that would genuinely help people,
that's worth raising.

This document explains how contributions work and what the bar is
for additions to the repository.

---

## How to Propose Something New

**Open an issue before writing anything.**

The most useful thing you can do first is describe the gap you're
seeing and what you think would address it. A short issue that says
"Section X doesn't cover Y, and I think it should because Z" gives
the maintainer (me) a chance to respond before you invest time in writing
something that may not fit the direction of the repo.

Issues are also valuable on their own. If you notice something
missing, something unclear, or something that doesn't match your
experience — that feedback matters even if you don't have a proposed
solution. The incomplete sentence fixed in Issue #1 and the capacity
framework proposed in Issue #4 are both examples of contributions
that started as issues.

If after discussion the proposed addition makes sense, you're welcome
to submit a pull request.

## What Makes a Good Contribution

This repository has a specific scope and voice, and contributions
need to fit both.

### Scope

Everything here is practical and IR-adjacent. The audience is
security and incident response leaders — managers, team leads, and
senior practitioners in high-stakes technical environments. Content
that works well here is:

* Grounded in how incident response actually operates, not how it
  theoretically should
* Actionable — something a manager can use in a 1:1, a rotation
  review, or a post-incident conversation
* Honest about the structural nature of burnout, rather than
  framing it as an individual problem

Content that is likely not a fit: general workplace wellness advice,
content aimed at non-technical teams, clinical or diagnostic
resources, and anything that could be found in a standard HR handbook.

### Attribution

If your contribution draws on a specific framework, research, or
external source, name it. The capacity decision framework in
`templates/capacity-response-protocol.md` is a good example — it
credits Woods and Hollnagel's work in *Joint Cognitive Systems:
Patterns in Cognitive Systems Engineering* because that's where the
framework comes from. Attribution matters here for the same reason
it matters in the toolkit itself: invisible work should be named.

## Submitting a Pull Request

Once you've opened an issue and had a conversation about the
proposed addition:

1. Fork the repository and create a branch for your change.
2. Follow the formatting conventions of the existing files — hard
   line wrapping at approximately 70 characters, `*` for bullets,
   consistent header levels. The repository is read in both rendered
   and raw form, and formatting matters in both.
3. For new files, add the standard header used across all resources:
   the title, subtitle, and the "Part of the Resilience by Design
   toolkit" attribution line with a link back to the README.
4. For new files, add a Connected Resources section at the bottom
   that links to the most relevant existing materials.
5. Add the standard license footer:
   *This work is licensed under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0
   International (CC BY 4.0).*
6. Open the pull request with a brief description of what you're
   adding and a reference to the issue it addresses.

---

## Corrections and Small Fixes

Typos, broken links, incomplete sentences, and factual corrections
don't need an issue first. A pull request with a clear description
of what you're fixing is sufficient :) 

---

## What Happens After You Submit

I will review all pull requests. Expect a response within
a reasonable window — this is a practitioner-maintained repository,
not a full-time project. If your contribution needs revisions, you'll
receive specific feedback. If it's accepted, you'll be acknowledged
in the CHANGELOG and, where appropriate, in the file itself.

Contributions that significantly shape the direction of the
repository — like the capacity decision framework proposed in
Issue #4 — will be credited by name and linked back to the
originating issue.

---

## A Note on Scope Decisions

Not every good idea belongs in this repository. Sometimes the right
answer to a proposed addition is "this is valuable but not a fit
here" — and that's not a judgment on the quality of the thinking.
The repository stays useful by staying focused. If your contribution
doesn't land here, that doesn't mean it shouldn't exist somewhere.

---

## Questions

If you're unsure whether something is worth raising, raise it anyway.
An issue that doesn't go anywhere is still better than a gap that
persists because nobody said anything.
