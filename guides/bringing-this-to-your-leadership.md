# Bringing This to Your Leadership

## A Guide for Individual Contributors and Senior Responders

**Part of the [Resilience by Design](../README.md) toolkit**

---

## Who This Is For

Not everyone reading the Resilience by Design materials is a manager. 
Many of you are individual contributors, senior responders, or team 
leads who recognize the patterns described in the toolkit and the 
article series—because you're living them.

You see the load concentrating on the same people. You feel the 
recovery time shrinking between incidents. You notice that rotations 
aren't really protecting anyone. You know that "just this once" has 
become the default. You might be one of the people carrying more 
context than anyone realizes, or watching a teammate quietly burn 
out while everything looks stable on paper.

And you want something to change, but you're not sure how to bring 
it up—or whether it would even be heard.

This guide is for you. It's designed to help you translate what 
you're experiencing into a conversation that your leadership can 
engage with, framed in terms that resonate with how leaders think 
about risk, performance, and team effectiveness.

This isn't about complaining or venting. It's about surfacing 
system-level feedback that your leadership may genuinely not have 
visibility into—and giving them something actionable to work with.

---

## Why This Conversation Is Hard

Before getting into how to have this conversation, it's worth naming 
why it's difficult.

**It can feel like admitting weakness.** In security and incident 
response culture, there's often an unspoken expectation that you 
handle pressure without complaint. Raising concerns about pace, 
load, or sustainability can feel like saying "I can't hack it"—even 
when what you're actually saying is "the system isn't set up to 
sustain this."

**It can feel disloyal.** If you respect your manager and your team, 
pointing out that things aren't working can feel like criticism of 
people you care about. It's not. Surfacing structural issues is one 
of the most valuable things a team member can do.

**It can feel pointless.** If you've raised concerns before and 
nothing changed, or if you've watched others raise concerns and get 
met with platitudes, it's reasonable to wonder whether it's worth 
the effort. This guide can't guarantee a response, but it can help 
you frame the conversation in a way that's harder to dismiss.

**It can feel risky.** Depending on your organization's culture, 
there may be real or perceived career risk in flagging sustainability 
concerns. Only you can assess that risk in your context. What this 
guide can do is help you frame the conversation around operational 
impact rather than personal struggle, which tends to be both safer 
and more effective.

## The Core Reframe

The single most important thing you can do when bringing this to 
leadership is **reframe what you're describing from a morale issue 
to an operational risk issue.**

Leaders in security understand risk. They plan for it, measure it, 
and build systems around it. When burnout or unsustainable pace is 
framed as "people are unhappy" or "morale is low," it competes with 
every other priority and often loses. When it's framed as a risk to 
decision quality, team resilience, and operational effectiveness, it 
lands in a category that leaders are already trained to take 
seriously.

This isn't manipulation. It's translation. You're describing the 
same reality in the language your audience is equipped to act on.

### Instead of This → Try This

**Instead of:** "The team is burned out."

**Try:** "I'm seeing patterns that suggest our current operating 
tempo is starting to affect decision quality and response 
effectiveness. I want to flag it before it becomes a bigger problem."

---

**Instead of:** "We need more people."

**Try:** "Our current capacity means that high-impact incidents are 
consistently handled by the same 2-3 people. If any of them were 
unavailable, we'd have a significant gap. I think we have a 
concentration risk that's worth looking at."

---

**Instead of:** "I'm exhausted."

**Try:** "I've been primary on the last four major incidents without 
a meaningful recovery period between them. I want to make sure my 
judgment stays sharp, and I'm not confident it will if the pace 
continues without a buffer."

---

**Instead of:** "Nobody appreciates the work we do."

**Try:** "A lot of the work that keeps the team effective—mentoring, 
prevention, follow-through after resolution—isn't captured in our 
current metrics. I think we might be under-recognizing the effort 
that prevents incidents, not just the effort that resolves them."

---

**Instead of:** "The on-call rotation doesn't work."

**Try:** "I've noticed that our rotation boundaries are being 
overridden frequently enough that they're not providing real 
recovery. I think the rotation structure might need a review to 
make sure it's actually protecting the team's sustained capacity."

---

**Instead of:** "People are going to leave."

**Try:** "I'm concerned about retention risk. Some of the patterns 
I'm seeing—declining engagement, shallow recovery between incidents, 
load concentration—are the same patterns that typically precede 
attrition in high-pressure teams. I think there are structural 
changes that could address this before it becomes a staffing problem."

---

The pattern in every reframe is the same: **name the observable 
pattern, connect it to an operational consequence, and suggest that 
a structural response is possible.** This gives your leader something 
to act on rather than something to feel bad about.

## Preparing for the Conversation

### Gather Specifics

Vague concerns are easy to dismiss. Specific observations are not. 
Before having this conversation, take some time to identify concrete 
data points.

**Patterns you've observed:**
- Who has been involved in the last several high-impact incidents? 
  Is it the same people?
- Have rotations been overridden? How often? For whom?
- Are people working after hours, during PTO, or outside their 
  assigned rotation? Can you point to specific instances?
- Has the team's approach to incidents changed? Are people defaulting 
  to familiar solutions rather than exploring better ones?
- Has anyone's engagement or participation visibly shifted?

**Your own experience:**
- How many high-impact incidents have you handled recently without 
  meaningful recovery?
- Are you carrying context or responsibilities that aren't officially 
  yours?
- When was the last time you genuinely disconnected from work?
- Is there work you're doing that nobody tracks or recognizes?

**Impact you've noticed:**
- Have you seen decision quality affected—shortcuts taken, details 
  missed, judgment calls that felt off?
- Has the team's capacity for proactive work (prevention, 
  improvement, learning) declined?
- Have you noticed teammates withdrawing, going quiet, or losing 
  the engagement they used to have?

You don't need to present all of this. But having specific examples 
ready means you can support your observations with evidence if 
asked, rather than relying on general impressions.

### Decide What You're Asking For

Going into the conversation with a clear sense of what you want 
makes it more productive. You don't need to have all the answers, 
but having a starting point helps your leader engage constructively 
rather than feeling like they need to solve an undefined problem.

Some possibilities:

- **Awareness:** "I want you to have visibility into what I'm 
  seeing, because I don't think it's showing up in our normal 
  metrics."

- **A specific change:** "I'd like us to review the rotation 
  structure" or "I'd like a recovery buffer after the next major 
  incident" or "I'd like us to look at how escalations are being 
  routed."

- **A broader conversation:** "I think the team would benefit from 
  a conversation about sustainable pace, and I'm hoping you'd be 
  open to facilitating that."

- **Personal relief:** "I need a lighter load for the next few 
  weeks to recover from what I've been carrying" or "I need to 
  not be the default escalation point for a while."

- **Structural review:** "I think some of the patterns I'm 
  describing are systemic. Would you be open to looking at this 
  through something like a rotation audit or a team resilience 
  review?"

It's okay to ask for more than one thing. It's also okay to start 
with awareness and see how the conversation develops.

### Choose Your Moment

Timing matters. Some suggestions:

- **A scheduled 1:1 is usually the best venue.** It's private, it's 
  expected, and there's already space for this kind of conversation.

- **Don't bring it up during an active incident.** The middle of a 
  crisis is not the time for a structural conversation. Wait until 
  there's space to think.

- **Shortly after a demanding incident is often effective.** The 
  experience is fresh, the cost is recent, and the connection 
  between what happened and what needs to change is clear.

- **Don't wait for the perfect moment.** If you keep deferring 
  because "now isn't the right time," recognize that the right 
  time may never arrive on its own. A good enough moment is 
  sufficient.

## Having the Conversation

### Opening

Start by framing why you're raising this. Establishing intent early 
prevents the conversation from being heard as a complaint.

Something like:

> "I want to talk about something I've been noticing on the team. 
> I'm not raising it as a complaint—I'm raising it because I think 
> there are some patterns that could become a real risk if they 
> continue, and I want to flag them while there's still time to 
> do something about it."

Or:

> "I've been thinking about our team's sustainability. Not in an 
> abstract way—I've noticed some specific things that I think are 
> worth discussing. Can I share what I'm seeing?"

Or, if it's more personal:

> "I want to be honest with you about where I'm at. I've been 
> carrying a pace that I don't think is sustainable, and I'd rather 
> flag it now than wait until it affects my work."

### During the Conversation

**Lead with observations, not conclusions.** "I've been primary on 
four of the last six major incidents" is harder to argue with than 
"the workload is unfair." Let your leader draw conclusions from the 
data you present.

**Connect observations to operational impact.** After sharing what 
you've noticed, make the link to why it matters: decision quality, 
response effectiveness, retention risk, concentration risk. This is 
the reframe from earlier in this guide.

**Be specific about what you're asking for.** Even if it's just 
"I wanted you to be aware of this," say that explicitly. If you 
have a specific request, make it.

**Acknowledge complexity.** Your leader is probably dealing with 
constraints you can't see—budget, organizational pressure, staffing 
limitations. Acknowledging that you understand they may not be able 
to fix everything immediately builds trust and keeps the conversation 
collaborative.

Something like:

> "I know there are constraints I might not have visibility into. 
> I'm not expecting you to solve this today. But I think it's 
> important that you know what I'm seeing from where I sit."

**Don't apologize for raising it.** This is not a complaint. It's 
operational feedback. You're doing your leader a service by making 
the invisible visible.

### If It Doesn't Go Well

Not every leader will respond the way you hope. Some possible 
responses and how to handle them:

**"Everyone's dealing with this right now."**

This normalizes the problem instead of addressing it. You can 
respond with: "I understand that. My concern is that if it's 
affecting everyone, that makes it a structural issue rather than 
an individual one—which means it probably needs a structural 
response."

**"We just need to get through this quarter."**

This defers action indefinitely, because there will always be 
another quarter. You can respond with: "I've heard that before, 
and I understand the pressure. But I want to make sure we're not 
building a pattern where 'getting through it' becomes the 
permanent operating model."

**"What do you want me to do about it?"**

This can feel dismissive but may be genuine. If you've prepared, 
you have an answer: "Here's what I think would help..." Reference 
the specific asks you identified earlier.

**"I appreciate you raising it, but I don't see the same thing."**

This is where your specific observations matter. "Here's what I'm 
basing this on..." and share the concrete data points you prepared.

**Silence or deflection.**

If the conversation doesn't land at all, you've still done 
something important: you've created a record that the concern was 
raised. If things deteriorate further, you can reference this 
conversation. And sometimes feedback that seems to bounce off in 
the moment takes root over time.

## If You're Not Ready for a Direct Conversation

Not everyone is in a position to have this conversation directly, 
and that's okay. Some alternatives:

**Share the article series or toolkit.** Sometimes the most 
effective thing you can do is forward a resource that says what 
you've been thinking. A message like "I read this and it resonated 
with some of what I've been experiencing on our team—curious what 
you think" is a low-risk way to open the door.

**Talk to a peer first.** If you're not sure whether what you're 
seeing is a pattern or just your own experience, talk to a trusted 
teammate. If they're seeing the same things, you have validation—and 
you may decide to raise it together, which can carry more weight.

**Use existing feedback channels.** If your organization has 
anonymous feedback mechanisms, engagement surveys, or skip-level 
conversations, use them. Frame your input using the same operational 
risk language from this guide.

**Document what you're seeing.** Even if you don't raise it now, 
keeping a private record of specific observations (dates, patterns, 
incidents, load distribution) means you'll have concrete evidence 
if and when you're ready to have the conversation.

## A Note on Self-Advocacy

There's a version of this guide that's about advocating for the 
team—surfacing structural patterns that affect everyone. That 
matters and it's most of what's above.

But there's also a version that's about advocating for yourself. 
And that's often harder.

If you're the person carrying more context than anyone else, the 
person who never fully disconnects, the person who absorbs the 
hardest cases because nobody else will—you are allowed to say that 
this costs something. You are allowed to ask for a different 
distribution. You are allowed to need recovery that's more than a 
weekend.

Flagging your own limits isn't a failure. In a well-functioning 
system, it's exactly what should happen. If it doesn't feel safe 
to do that in your current environment, that's important information 
about the environment—not about you.

You can't pour from a nearly empty cup indefinitely, no matter 
how good you are at making it look full.
